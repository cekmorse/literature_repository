On the Treatment of Evapotranspiration, Soil Moisture Accounting, and Aquifer Recharge in Monthly Water Balance Models

William M. Alley

Abstract

Several two- to six-parameter regional water balance models are examined by using 50-year records of monthly streamflow at 10 sites in New Jersey. These models include variants of the Thornthwaite-Mather model, the Palmer model, and the more recent Thomas abcd model. Prediction errors are relatively similar among the models. However, simulated values of state variables such as soil moisture storage differ substantially among the models, and fitted parameter values for different models sometimes indicated an entirely different type of basin response to precipitation. Some problems in parameter identification are noted, including difficulties in identifying an appropriate time lag factor for the Thornthwaite-Mather-type model for basins with little groundwater storage, very high correlations between upper and lower storages in the Palmer-type model, and large sensitivity of parameter a of the abcd model to bias in estimates of precipitation and potential evapotranspiration. Modifications to the threshold concept of the Thornthwaite-Mather model were statistically valid for the six stations in northern New Jersey. The abcd model resulted in a simulated seasonal cycle of groundwater levels similar to fluctuations observed in nearby wells but with greater persistence. These results suggest that extreme caution should be used in attaching physical significance to model parameters and in using the state variables of the models in indices of drought and basin productivity.

INTRODUCTION
Water balance models were developed in the 1940's by
Thornthwaite [1948-] and later revised by Thornthwaite and
Mather [1955•. These models are essentially bookkeeping pro-
cedures which estimate the balance between the income of
water from precipitation and snowmelt and the outflow of
water by evapotranspiration, streamflow, and groundwater re-
charge. Water balances have been used for such purposes as
computing the seasonal and geographic patterns of irrigation
demand and the soil moisture stresses under which crops and
vegetation can survive. They have also been used for predict-
ing streamflow, lake levels, depths to groundwater, and the
hydrologic effects of weather modification or of changes in
vegetation cover [Dunne and Leopold, 1978-]. Water balances
have been computed for both soil profiles and drainage basins
and have been computed on daily, weekly, monthly, and even
annual bases. This paper addresses regional water balance
models with a monthly time step.
One use of these models is to reconstruct a period of runoff
at an ungaged site or a site with a short record of measured
streamflow and a long record of precipitation and temper-
ature. However, other record reconstruction techniques, ex-
ploiting correlations among streamflow stations [Fiering,
1962•, are more commonly used. On the other hand, Mather
[1981, p. 474] notes that
Study of the basins where agreement between computed and
measured [runoff] values is only fair to poor reveals that the
patterns of disagreement can be used to provide information on
the water-holding capacity in the root zone of the soil, on the
quantity of deep aquifer recharge within the basin, or on the
effect of human modifications within the basin.
He also notes that water balances can reveal the quantity of
interbasin transfers or other consumptive uses within the
basin. Thomas [1981] suggests that water balance models

might be used as an independent check on consumptive use
estimates computed from deterministic analyses of water

Water balances may offer a means of removing climatic varia-
bility from monthly or annual flow records such that trends in
streamflow are more readily detectable.
Thomas [1981] and Thomas et al. [1983] suggest that the
state variables simulated in a water balance may be useful in
themselves. They note that ecologists and other biologists are
often more interested in what happens within a drainage basin
than in the export of water from it. For example, soil moisture
and evapotranspiration together may be useful surrogates for
the basin's performance with regard to flora and fauna.
Thomas et al. [1983] suggest that the impact of a succession of
dry years on basin biota can be assessed more accurately by
deviations from norms of evaporation and residual moisture
than by deviations from mean annual rainfall or runoff.
Palmer [1965] used a monthly water balance model to devel-
op an index of meteorological drought.
This paper investigates the use of regional water balance
models to transform monthly precipitation and monthly po-
tential evapotranspiration data to monthly and annual runoff
estimates. Fifty-year records of monthly streamflow at 10 sta-
tions in New Jersey are used in the analysis. Unfortunately, a
universal philosophy or approach for comparing hydrologic
models does not exist, and optimality in parameter space does
not imply optimality in decision space [Marin, 1983]. Here, I
explore four major issues which have received little or no
attention with regard to these models. First, are the identified
parameter values reasonable and consistent between models?
Second, do any of the models result in substantially lower
prediction errors than the others and are calibration errors a
reliable indicator of prediction errors? Third, how accurately
can the parameter values be estimated and what are their
covariances ? Finally, what is the relationship of modeled state
variables which are related to groundwater storage to actual
measurements of water levels in wells? Answers to these
questions are important for assessing the value of regional
monthly water balance models in their above-mentioned ap-
plications.

Relationship of Actual and Potential
E vapotr anspir ation
Most water balance models assume that evapotranspiration
for a period is equal to the potential evapotranspiration when-
ever Pi-> PEi. This assumption is usually made regardless of
whether one is performing a daily, weekly, or monthly water
balance. However, precipitation and evapotranspiration often
are distributed within a month in such a way that both
periods of deficiency and surplus can occur.
Evapotranspiration during months with P, _> PE, is hand-
led differently by the abcd model. Except for the trivial case
when PE, = 0.0, the model will always assume that E• < PE•.
Also, it is not uncommon for the abcd model to simulate a
decrease in soil moisture storage even though P• _> PE,. These
characteristics of the abcd model are primarily due to the fact
that an upper limit is placed on the sum of evapotranspiration
and soil moisture storage rather than on simply soil moisture
storage.
When PEi < Pi and soil moisture deficits develop, almost
all water balance models invoke some limitation on evapo-
transpiration as a function of soil moisture content. The avail-
ability of soil moisture for plant growth over the range from
field capacity to permanent wilting point has been treated by a
wide range of techniques. At one end of the spectrum, Veih-
meyer and Hendrickson [1955] suggested that in some cases
evapotranspiration may proceed at the potential rate until soil
moisture approaches the permanent wilting point. On the
other hand, Thornthwaite and Mather [1955] assume the ratio
of actual to potential evapotranspiration is a linear function of
the ratio of available soil moisture to the available water ca-
pacity. However, it is not generally recognized that by as-
suming that Ei- PE, whenever Pi _> PE•, Thornthwaite and
Mather [1955] are implicitly assuming that, during certain
months, evapotranspiration occurs at the potential rate even
though a soil moisture deficit (D• > 0) may exist.
The true relationship between actual and potential evapo-
transpiration will vary with rooting characteristics, soil tex-
ture, and plant physiology, as well as with the rate of evapo-
transpiration itself and the climatological conditions. A popu-
lar compromise between the above two models has been the
use of a so-called "root constant" [Penman, 1949]. Evapotran-
spiration is assumed to occur at the potential rate until the
soil moisture deficit D• exceeds the root constant; then evapo-
transpiration proceeds at a slower rate. Palmer's model is an
example of a model that uses a root constant in the form of
parameter •,. Palmer's model uses an analog of the linear
approach of Thornthwaite and Mather [1955] to estimate eva-
potranspiration from the lower layer. Another approach is to
assume simply that evapotranspiration losses from the lower
layer are equal to some percentage (often of the order of 10%)
of the potential loss [for example, Calder et al., 1983; Howard
and Lloyd, 1979; Rushton and Ward, 1979].
The amount of evapotranspiration, and thus the long-term
balance between runoff and evapotranspiration, is modeled by
• in the type T model, •, and •b in the P model, and a and b
in the abcd model. A useful measure of this balance is the
sensitivity of the simulated mean annual runoff to the various
model parameters.
If parameter • is increased in the T model, then the general
result is less runoff. This occurs because, for the same soil

parameter a using the two different objective functions were
very close, generally within 0.002 of each other. Bias in esti-

network of historical gages is used. Thus parameter a may be
unrepresentative of "the propensity of runoff to occur before
the soil is fully saturated."
Soil Moisture Accounting and Aquifer
Recharge
Simulated soil moisture storage at the end of a month was
more often than not at its capacity in the T or P models. In
the abcd model, soil moisture storage at the end of a month is
only at the capacity b when a- 1.0 and evaporation for the
month is zero. These differences in results may have important
implications if soil moisture storage is used as part of a
drought index or index of productivity within a drainage
basin.
The T and P models rely heavily on the threshold concept
that runoff or recharge does not occur until soil moisture
capacity is filled. The limitations of this assumption have been
recently reviewed by Morton [1983]. When the same values of
ß (or •a and •b) are used, estimates of recharge will tend to
decrease as the time step of the computation increases. For
example, Rushton and Ward [1979] found that for T type
models, monthly water balances lead to recharge values which
are up to 25% less than those from daily water balances.
Several studies summarized by Rushton and Ward [1979] sug-
gest that the threshold-type models tend to underestimate re-
charge during the summer and early autumn. Even if the
threshold-type models were correct for a unit soil column,
other factors that might contribute to underestimates of
summer or early autumn recharge include temporal aggrega-
tion of precipitation as a monthly average, spatial aggregation
of precipitation, and spatial aggregation of soil moisture ca-
pacity and root constants as average values.

PARAMETER ESTIMATION
Often the T model has been applied with estimates of (I)
based on soil texture and rooting characteristics of the veg-

The most commonly adopted objective function for hydro-

residuals versus the computed flows suggested this was a rea-

residuals which will be discussed subsequently.

APPLICATION OF THE MODELS
The various models were investigated using 50-year records
of monthly streamflow at 10 sites in New Jersey. These sta-
tions are listed in Table 1 along with some of their basin and
flow characteristics. Streamflow records at the 10 sites are
relatively unaffected by man's activities and thus are assumed
to be stationary time series. Stations 1 to 6 are located in the
northern part of the state and drain a fairly diverse set of
geologic environments. Stations 7 to 10 are all located in the
coastal plain of New Jersey. Drainage basins for these stations
are underlain by relatively thick sequences of unconsolidated
sediments. Flow in coastal plain streams tends to be much less
variable than in the northern streams, as indicated by the flow
characteristic DUR in Table 1; DUR is the ratio of the flow
equaled or exceeded 10% of the time to that flow equaled or
exceeded 90% of the time. High values of DUR suggest a
highly variable stream whose time lag between precipitation
and runoff is relatively short. Low values of DUR suggest a
stream with considerable flow contributions from groundwa-
ter discharge and relatively long time lags between precipi-
tation and runoff.
Precipitation and temperature data used in the study were
divisional average monthly values readily available from the
National Weather Service from 1931 to the present. The
analysis of stations 1 to 6 used data from climatic division 1,
while data from climatic division 2 were used for stations 7 to
10. Potential evapotranspiration was estimated using
Thornthwaite's method [Thornthwaite, 1948]. Other rain gage
networks for estimating monthly precipitation and other
methods for estimating monthly potential evapotranspiration
could have been used. However, the data selected appeared
satisfactory for this analysis, were easy to obtain, and resulted
in verification errors and correlations between simulated and
measured streamflow similar to those commonly reported for
these models.
